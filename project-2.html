What Venture Capital Gets Wrong About AI Scaling

Article Business Artificial Intelligence

Last Updated: 21 October 2025

Venture capital matured in an era when the growth of software companies followed a reassuring pattern. A firm would spend heavily to develop a product and bring it to market, but once the work was done the cost of serving each additional customer was relatively small. As sales increased, the margins improved. Over time, this pattern hardened into expectation and investors came to assume that scale would steadily reduce the relative weight of costs.

Artificial intelligence does not always behave in this way. Many AI systems, particularly those deployed in finance, healthcare, or other regulated fields, remain costly long after their initial release. Each new customer adds to the demand for computing power, infrastructure and technical oversight. Models must be observed carefully to ensure that their outputs remain consistent and defensible, especially where errors may expose clients to legal or reputational harm. And as data evolves, performance can drift, which in turn requires retraining, recalibration and further supervision.

The result is that growth does not necessarily lighten the enterprise. In some AI businesses, rising usage brings rising operating expense. Revenue expands, but so does the infrastructure required to sustain it. The company becomes larger and more complex at the same time. This creates an awkward tension when such firms are evaluated using venture benchmarks designed for an earlier generation of software companies. Rapid revenue growth is ordinarily treated as a signal that efficiency will soon follow. In many AI ventures, however, increasing revenue reflects heavier computational demand and expanding layers of oversight. A startup may appear to be thriving commercially while struggling to deliver the widening margins investors have been trained to expect.

Strategic behaviour then adjusts accordingly. Founders feel pressure to emphasise autonomy and automation even when prudent deployment requires caution and human review. Investors, for their part, may interpret operational discipline as a lack of ambition rather than as an acknowledgment of structural cost. Neither side is irrational. Both are simply operating within expectations shaped by a different technological era.

Not all AI businesses face this constraint. Some consumer tools and lower-risk applications may scale in ways that resemble traditional software. But many of the most consequential uses of AI are embedded within existing institutions, where reliability and accountability revolve around the product itself. In such settings, oversight is not a temporary burden but a permanent feature of the business model.

The difficulty, then, is not that AI lacks transformative potential. It is that much of it operates under an economic logic that differs from the one venture capital was built to fund. Investors learned to reward companies that grew lighter as they expanded but many AI firms grow heavier. Until those expectations adjust, the mismatch will persist.
