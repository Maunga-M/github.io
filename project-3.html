Power, Patronage and Politically Exposed Persons: The Case of the Gupta Network in South Africa

Case Study Financial Crime & Governance

Last Updated: 14th March 2025

For several years, South Africa experienced what was widely characterised as state capture. The name “Gupta” moved from business reporting to the centre of national political debate. Companies linked to the Gupta family secured state contracts and channelled billions of rands through the formal banking system. By the time law enforcement began to respond meaningfully, key actors had already left the jurisdiction. The scandal continues to raise difficult questions about whether modern financial oversight systems can withstand sustained political influence.

From Enterprise to Influence

Ajay, Atul and Rajesh Gupta arrived in South Africa from India in the 1990s and established business interests across media, mining, technology and engineering. By the mid-2000s and into the 2010s, companies linked to the family were securing substantial contracts with South African state-owned enterprises, including Eskom, the national power utility, and Transnet, the freight and logistics company responsible for transporting coal, iron ore and containerised goods across the country.

Through Oakbay Investments and a network of affiliated entities, the family cultivated close relationships with senior political office holders, most notably President Jacob Zuma. Over time, investigative reporting and the findings of the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture (the Zondo Commission) concluded that these relationships extended beyond conventional political patronage. They involved systematic efforts to influence public appointments, procurement outcomes and redirect public resources for private benefit.

State contracts worth billions were alleged to have flowed through Gupta-linked entities and, in many instances, through layered offshore structures. Importantly, much of this money moved through regulated banks within South Africa’s formal financial system, rather than outside it.

The Banks Cut Ties

South Africa does not lack financial sophistication. Its banking sector is widely regarded as one of the most developed and well-regulated on the African continent. It also operates within a comprehensive anti-money-laundering framework aligned with international standards. Financial institutions are legally obliged to verify clients, identify politically exposed persons and submit suspicious transaction reports where appropriate. The system is designed to detect irregular financial movement within the formal economy. In the case of companies within the Gupta business network, those obligations were triggered repeatedly.

Between December 2012 and June 2016, banks submitted seventy-two suspicious transaction reports relating to Gupta-linked entities. The scale of those reported transactions only became clearer later, during court filings in October 2016, which revealed that a certificate issued by the Financial Intelligence Centre indicated that the transactions in question amounted to approximately R6.8 billion. Although the reports had been submitted over several years, there is no public record of successful criminal prosecution or asset forfeiture during that period in relation to those specific transactions.

During this same period, several institutions reassessed their exposure. In November 2014, Absa’s politically exposed persons committee resolved to terminate its relationship with Oakbay Investments, the Gupta family’s principal holding company, citing unexplained transfers and escalating risk. Formal closures followed in December 2015 and early 2016. Nedbank and FNB issued notice in April 2016 and terminated services shortly thereafter. Standard Bank, after filing suspicious transaction reports, notified Oakbay that it would also end its relationship.

These were commercial risk decisions taken by the banks themselves. Public records do not indicate that the closures were mandated by regulatory enforcement at that stage. When the decisions became public, Cabinet established an inter-ministerial committee to examine them and ministers met with financial institutions. The banks did not reverse their decisions, and the courts later confirmed that government lacked the authority to compel private institutions to maintain banking relationships.

Adaptation and Collapse

The withdrawal of the major domestic banks did not immediately halt the movement of funds. Instead, Gupta-linked entities consolidated their banking activities through the South African branch of Bank of Baroda, which allowed the network’s financial operations to continue despite mounting criticism.

A key illustration of this shift involved business deals with the national power utility Eskom. In April 2016, Eskom's board approved a prepayment of over R600 million to the mining company Tegeta, which was controlled by the Gupta family, for future coal deliveries to the Majuba power station. Evidence later presented to the Zondo Commission indicated that this prepayment formed part of the funding structure that enabled Tegeta to complete its acquisition of Optimum Coal Mine. Money from this deal flowed into the Gupta's Baroda accounts. According to the Organised Crime and Corruption Reporting Project (OCCRP), Eskom transferred more than R1.8 billion into multiple accounts belonging to the Gupta family at Bank of Baroda between January 2016 and February 2017, .

Regulatory inspections later identified compliance deficiencies at Bank of Baroda’s South African branch under the Financial Intelligence Centre Act, including failures relating to customer due diligence and reporting obligations. In 2017, the South African Reserve Bank imposed an administrative penalty of R11 million, which was significantly reduced to R400,000 on appeal. Later that year, the bank announced that it would close Gupta-linked accounts and withdraw its operations from South Africa.

In February 2018, President Jacob Zuma resigned amid mounting political and legal pressure linked to the state-capture allegations. Shortly thereafter, the Gupta brothers fled to Dubai. Their exit coincided with intensifying investigations and a political climate that had become less protective of their network. In June 2022, the Gupta brothers were arrested in the United Arab Emirates pursuant to Interpol red notices linked to South African charges of fraud, corruption and money laundering. South Africa’s subsequent extradition request for Atul and Rajesh Gupta was dismissed by a UAE court in early 2023, which held that jurisdictional and procedural requirements had not been satisfied.

Regulating the Politically Connected

The international pursuit of the Gupta brothers addressed only one dimension of the problem. The broader challenge lay in how government institutions managed the intersection of political power and commercial activity, particularly in relation to financial crime. The controversy surrounding the bank closures in 2016 revealed a deeper tension within the anti-money-laundering framework. The issue was not only whether suspicious transactions had been reported, but how financial institutions were expected to treat clients whose influence derived from proximity to political power.

Rules governing politically exposed persons exist because power attracts illicit capital. When an individual holds prominent public office, the risk of corruption increases and enhanced safeguards are meant to follow. The Gupta network’s advantage did not arise primarily from technical loopholes or weak controls, but from its close association with political authority. Its businesses operated alongside senior office holders, most notably President Jacob Zuma, whose administration oversaw the state-owned enterprises that awarded lucrative contracts to Gupta-linked companies.

Anti-money laundering frameworks in many jurisdictions distinguish between foreign and domestic political exposure. Foreign politically exposed persons are typically treated as inherently higher risk and are automatically subject to enhanced due diligence measures, including senior management approval, source-of-wealth verification and intensified ongoing monitoring. Domestic politically exposed persons, by contrast, are often assessed under a risk-based approach rather than being categorised as high risk by default. Financial institutions are required to evaluate the specific circumstances of the relationship and determine whether enhanced monitoring is warranted based on their own assessment of risk. In principle, this distinction reflects ideas of sovereignty and proportionality. Foreign exposure carries cross-border corruption risks that are presumed higher, while domestic exposure is evaluated within the context of local oversight systems. In practice, however, applying a discretionary approach to powerful domestic actors or those closely associated with them can create structural challenges that do not arise in ordinary commercial relationships.

First, there is an asymmetry of power. Senior political figures and their associates often possess access to legal expertise, advisory networks and complex corporate structures that distance them from the proceeds of questionable transactions. Funds may move through layered entities or offshore arrangements that are lawful on paper but difficult to keep track of. Establishing criminal intent in such circumstances requires sustained investigative capacity and institutional stability. Second, enforcement decisions carry consequences. Escalating monitoring or pursuing action against politically connected individuals may trigger institutional resistance or political backlash. The reaction of cabinet ministers to the 2016 bank closures demonstrated how compliance decisions involving state-linked clients could quickly escalate beyond internal risk processes and into political confrontation.

The Gupta case demonstrates that technical reporting mechanisms can function while decisive enforcement against politically connected networks remains difficult to sustain. These dynamics do not imply that anti-money-laundering systems are inherently ineffective or doomed to fail in political cases. They illustrate that such systems depend on independence and insulation from political pressure. Where those conditions are fragile, the application of rules to powerful insiders becomes uneven.

Author's Note

I carried this case study to better understand the gap that can emerge between regulation and enforcement in practice. The Gupta state capture was one of the most consequential governance scandals in South Africa’s recent history, and its scale made for an even more interesting study. It offered very good example of how political proximity can test even strong financial systems. Through this analysis, I developed a deeper appreciation for the structural challenges faced by institutions tasked with applying anti-money-laundering controls to politically connected actors.
