Financial Statement Analysis - Airtel Zambia (2024)

Finance Business

Last Updated: 18 December 2025

Airtel Networks Zambia Plc is one of Zambia’s major telecommunications operators and has a customer base of over 11 million. The company provides mobile voice, data, and mobile financial services to both retail and corporate clients. It operates as part of the Airtel Africa Group and has been listed on the Lusaka Securities Exchange since 2008.

This project presents a financial statement analysis of Airtel Zambia based on its 2024 annual report. It's purpose is to evaluate the company’s financial performance using information from the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement. The analysis interprets the reported figures to provide a clear view of Airtel Zambia’s financial position and performance during the 2024 financial year.

The full annual report can be accessed here: https://www.airtel.co.zm/home/annualreportzm

Executive Summary

In 2024, Airtel Zambia recorded revenue growth of 26%, supported by continued expansion of its customer base and network infrastructure. Profit after tax increased by 10%, confirming that the company remains strongly profitable. However, both operating and net profit margins declined slightly due to higher network costs, increased depreciation, rising finance expenses, and foreign exchange losses.

Liquidity remains constrained, as reflected in the low current ratio and the net current liability position. This aligns with the company’s prepaid business model, which generates strong operating cash flows and results in a negative operating cycle. Cash generation therefore remains solid.

Leverage is still elevated, although net debt declined during the year. Higher finance costs and weaker interest cover point to increased exposure to interest rate and currency risk. The company's operational efficiency improved meaningfully in 2024, with infrastructure investments being utilised more effectively and working capital tightly managed. Dividend payouts remain high relative to earnings, although total dividends recognised during the year declined compared to 2023.

Overall, the company is profitable and generates strong cash flows, but its high leverage and margin pressure mean that continued earnings stability is essential to maintain financial strength.

Profitability

Airtel Zambia delivered strong top-line growth in 2024, with revenue rising 26% from K5.6 billion to K7.1 billion. However, profit after tax increased by a more modest 10%, from K1.1 billion to K1.25 billion. This gap between revenue growth and profit growth indicates that additional sales were generated at thinner margins.

Operating profit increased from K2,276 million to K2,637 million. However, the operating margin declined from approximately 40% in 2023 to 37% in 2024. Since operating profit is generated from revenue less operating expenses (including network costs, sales and marketing, licence fees, and depreciation), this decline in margin indicates that operating costs have increased at a faster rate than revenue.

At the financing level, profit before tax increased to K1.95 billion. However, finance costs rose sharply from K429 million to K551 million, and foreign exchange losses almost doubled to K138 million. These higher financing and currency costs absorbed a significant portion of the additional operating profit.As a result, the net profit margin declined from 20% to 18%. The business remains highly profitable, but the efficiency of converting revenue into final earnings has weakened slightly.

Return on Capital Employed (ROCE) improved significantly from 58.5% to 74.3%, driven by higher operating profit and a reduction in net debt.[1] This indicates that the capital invested in the business is being used more intensively. Return on Equity (ROE), on the other hand, declined from 259% to 222%.[2] Although profits increased, the growth in equity diluted the overall return percentage. Even so, ROE remains exceptionally high and reflects the company’s leveraged capital structure

With finance costs of K551 million on borrowings of about K3.1 billion, the effective finance cost rate (including lease interest) is approximately 17–18%.[3] Given that ROCE stands at 74.3%, the company continues to generate returns well above its borrowing cost, creating economic value despite rising finance expenses.

Overall, Airtel achieved strong revenue growth in 2024, but this growth came with margin pressure and higher financing risk. The company remains profitable and cash-generative, but sustaining this performance will require careful control of operating and financing costs going forward.

Liquidity

Despite strong profitability, Airtel Zambia’s balance sheet shows ongoing short-term liquidity pressure. At 31 December 2024, current assets stood at approximately K1 billion, while current liabilities totalled about K3.9 billion. This results in a current ratio of 0.26:1, down from 0.32:1 in 2023.[4] On the surface, this suggests that short-term obligations significantly exceed short-term assets. The company also reported a net current liability position of roughly K2.9 billion, compared to K2 billion in the prior year.[5] This indicates that short-term liabilities increased at a faster rate than short-term assets during 2024.

However, these figures must be interpreted within the context of Airtel’s operating model. The company operates largely on a prepaid basis, meaning cash is often received before services are delivered. This leads to contract liabilities rather than trade receivables and results in a structurally low current ratio. In effect, the business collects cash upfront and recognises revenue later. Operating cash flow during the year was strong at approximately K3.1 billion, comfortably exceeding reported profit. This confirms effective cash conversion and supports the view that liquidity risk is operationally manageable despite the weak current ratio.

Trade and other receivables total approximately K414.7 million, of which K371.4 million relates to related-party balances. Net trade receivables are only about K43.4 million.Receivables represent only a small fraction of annual turnover, reflecting tight credit control and limited exposure to bad debts, consistent with the prepaid model.

The main liquidity pressure arises from short-term borrowings. Current maturities of loans and overdrafts amount to approximately K1.9 billion. This means the company depends heavily on refinancing and rollover of facilities. While management reports access to committed funding lines, this reliance introduces refinancing risk, particularly in a high interest rate environment.

It is also notable that dividends continue to be distributed despite the net current liability position. Although the business generates strong cash flows, ongoing dividend payments limit balance sheet strengthening and maintain reliance on external financing.

Leverage

At 31 December 2024, total borrowings, including overdrafts and lease liabilities, amounted to approximately K3.1 billion, down from K3.5 billion in 2023. Cash balances increased to about K145 million from K81 million. After deducting cash, net debt declined from roughly K3.45 billion to K3 billion. This reduction of about K460 million shows that the company reduced leverage during the year.

Total capital (equity plus net debt) stood at approximately K3.55 billion in 2024, compared to K3.89 billion in 2023. On this basis, the gearing ratio declined from 89% to 84%.[6] Although leverage remains high, the downward movement is positive. Equity remains relatively small at about K564 million compared to borrowings of over K3.1 billion. This means the business continues to rely heavily on debt financing. As a result, changes in interest rates, exchange rates, or profitability could have a significant impact on shareholder returns.

Interest cover declined from 5.3x to 4.8x.[7] The company still generates enough operating profit to meet its interest obligations comfortably. However, the decline reflects higher finance costs and reduces the margin of safety. If borrowing costs rise further, coverage could weaken.

Despite high leverage, current solvency appears manageable. Operating cash flow exceeded K3 billion during the year, net debt declined, and interest is covered nearly five times by operating profit. There were no reported breaches of loan covenants.

However, the capital structure carries ongoing risks. The company is exposed to interest rate movements on floating-rate debt, foreign exchange risk on USD-denominated borrowings, and refinancing risk due to large short-term maturities.

The company's capital structure is sustainable under current conditions, supported by strong cash generation. However, the high reliance on debt means that stability depends on continued earnings growth, controlled finance costs, and stable macroeconomic conditions.

Efficiency

The resources of a business are used to generate revenue and profit, and the efficiency with which those resources are deployed directly influences returns to investors. Efficiency ratios show how effectively Airtel Zambia uses its assets to generate revenue and manage working capital. These indicators help explain the movement in return on capital and cash generation during the year

Asset turnover increased from 1.02x in 2023 to 1.26x in 2024.[8] This means the company generated more revenue per kwacha invested in assets. The improvement is significant and indicates better utilisation of infrastructure and network assets. Although operating margins declined slightly, the stronger asset turnover more than offset this effect and contributed to the improvement in return on capital. In practical terms, investments made in prior years are now generating higher revenue.

Non-current assets account for approximately 82% of total assets, confirming that the business is highly capital intensive. The company relies heavily on network equipment, spectrum licences, and right-of-use assets to operate. Depreciation and amortisation totalled approximately K881 million and was equal to about 12% of revenue.[9] This highlights the ongoing reinvestment requirement in the telecommunications sector. Sustained growth depends not only on revenue expansion but also on continued capital expenditure to maintain and expand the network.

Inventory remains minimal relative to revenue. Inventory days declined from 2.2 days to 1.1 days.[10] This is consistent with a service-based model where physical inventory plays a limited role. Lower inventory days reduce holding costs and minimise the risk of obsolescence. Given the nature of the industry, inventory management is not a major driver of performance.

Receivables days declined from 4.9 days to 2.2 days.[11] This reflects the predominantly prepaid model and strong credit control. Cash is collected quickly, which supports operating cash flow and reduces reliance on external financing. The improvement reinforces earlier observations that earnings are efficiently converted into cash.

Payables days increased slightly from 77 days to 80 days, which means the company is taking slightly longer to pay suppliers.[12] A moderate extension of payment terms can provide short-term financing support without signalling financial difficulty. The increase appears controlled and contributes positively to working capital management.

The operating cycle remains strongly negative at approximately –77 days, compared to –70 days in 2023.[13] A negative cycle means the company receives cash from customers before paying suppliers. In practice, customers pay upfront for services through prepaid airtime and data, while supplier payments are settled later. As a result, the business does not need to finance working capital from its own cash or bank borrowings. This structural feature of the prepaid model supports liquidity, even though short-term liabilities exceed short-term assets.

Investor Ratios

Airtel Zambia distributed most of its earnings to shareholders in 2024, continuing its high payout policy. Earnings per share (EPS) increased from K10.95 in 2023 to K12.04 in 2024, reflecting the 10% growth in profit after tax.[14] As no new shares were issued, this increase was not diluted. Shareholders therefore benefited directly from higher earnings during the year.

Dividend per share (DPS) for 2024 is approximately K10.84, based on total dividends of K1.13 billion recognised during the year. This is lower than the K1.37 billion distributed in 2023.[15] Although earnings increased, total dividends recognised during 2024 declined, resulting in a modest improvement in dividend cover.

Dividend cover improved to approximately 1.11x from below 1x in 2023.[16] While this represents an improvement, the cover ratio remains low. Earnings only modestly exceed dividend distributions, leaving limited retained profit to reduce debt or strengthen equity. In a capital-intensive and highly leveraged business, such a low cover ratio constrains financial flexibility. Dividend sustainability therefore depends on continued stable earnings and strong cash generation.

Conclusion

Airtel Zambia remains a financially strong business, supported by robust cash generation and improving asset utilisation. The company continues to earn returns well above its cost of debt, which indicates that value is being created despite margin pressure.

However, the capital structure remains highly leveraged, and the near-100% dividend payout leaves limited retained earnings to strengthen the balance sheet. Financial resilience therefore depends on continued earnings stability and controlled financing costs. Exposure to interest rate and foreign exchange risk remains a key consideration.

For potential investors, the company offers strong current earnings and an attractive dividend stream. However, this return profile is accompanied by elevated financial risk due to leverage and limited dividend cover. The investment case therefore reflects a balance between strong cash generation and sensitivity to macroeconomic conditions.

Overall, the company’s performance in 2024 demonstrates operational strength, but its capital structure requires disciplined management to preserve long-term resilience.
